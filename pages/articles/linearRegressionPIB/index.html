Multiple Linear Regression: Activities with the Greatest Impact on GDP Growth in Mexico
Research Objective
To identify and quantify the sectors and activities with the greatest impact on national GDP growth in Mexico, in order to provide a model that can accurately estimate GDP under various economic conditions.

This analysis will provide valuable insights for policymakers and economists, guiding them on which sectors to prioritize to foster sustainable and productive economic growth, while also offering a tool for more precise GDP predictions.
Activities to Analyze
  1. Agriculture

    Agriculture is essential for the livelihood of many communities in Mexico. States like Sinaloa and Jalisco lead in corn and other crop production, while Chiapas stands out in coffee. This sector not only contributes to GDP but is also vital for food security.

  2. Mining

    Mining is a key sector, with states like Zacatecas and Durango being important producers of silver and other minerals. Additionally, Sonora is known for its copper production, an essential resource for the industry.

  3. Electric Power Generation and Distribution

    This sector is critical for economic development. Veracruz and Tamaulipas are states with significant energy infrastructures, playing a crucial role in electricity generation from both renewable and conventional sources.

  4. Construction

    Construction drives urban development, with Mexico City and the State of Mexico leading in infrastructure projects, including housing and public works.

  5. Food Industry

    The food industry not only meets domestic demand but also exports products. Puebla and Jalisco are recognized for their processed products, such as tequila and sauces.

  6. Beverage and Tobacco Industry

    States like Jalisco and Coahuila are significant in the production of alcoholic beverages, especially tequila. This industry generates employment and is a driver of exports.

  7. Machinery and Equipment Manufacturing

    The machinery industry is vital for manufacturing, with Querétaro and Guanajuato as development hubs. These regions are key for the production of agricultural and automotive machinery.

  8. Chemical Products Manufacturing

    The chemical industry is crucial for industrial development, with the State of Mexico and Veracruz standing out in the production of basic and specialized chemical products.

  9. Wholesale Trade

    Wholesale trade is a cornerstone of the tertiary sector. Mexico City is a central hub, with numerous markets and wholesalers supplying the country.

  10. Financial and Insurance Services

    This sector is fundamental for the economy, with Mexico City as the financial center. Banking and insurance institutions are essential for investment and economic development.

  11. Transportation and Storage

    This activity is crucial for trade and logistics in Mexico, facilitating the mobility of goods and people. States with the most activity in this sector include Veracruz, State of Mexico, and Nuevo León, where ports, airports, and land transport networks are concentrated.

  12. Health and Social Assistance Services

    Health is a fundamental pillar for the well-being of the population, and its development directly impacts productivity and quality of life. States like Jalisco, Mexico City, and Guanajuato have well-established and constantly growing health infrastructure.

  13. Educational Services

    Education is key for the development of human capital and innovation. States like Mexico City, Nuevo León, and Jalisco have a high concentration of educational institutions, from primary schools to universities, contributing to the formation of a skilled workforce.

  14. Temporary Accommodation Services

    This sector is essential for tourism, which is one of the main sources of income in Mexico. States like Quintana Roo, Mexico City, and Baja California Sur stand out for their hotel infrastructure and tourism services, attracting millions of visitors every year.

  15. Repair and Maintenance Services

    This sector contributes to the sustainability of goods and equipment, promoting more conscious and efficient consumption. Industrial cities like Monterrey, Tijuana, and Guadalajara are key centers for this type of service, benefiting both businesses and consumers.

Data Download and Selection

The data was extracted using the API provided by INEGI, specifically from the Indicators Bank under "GDP and National Accounts", focusing on the "Quarterly Gross Domestic Product by Economic Activity (in millions of pesos at 2013 prices)" for the Mexican economy, covering data from the first quarter of 1993 to the first quarter of 2023. This provided a comprehensive set of quarterly observations.

However, to align with the objective of estimating the national GDP while considering current economic relationships, it was necessary to narrow the time frame to capture more recent patterns and trends. Recognizing potential structural and economic shifts over the last three decades, the dataset was refined to include data from the second quarter of 2008 onwards, encompassing the last 15 years up to the most recent available period (first quarter of 2023). This selection of 60 quarters enables the model to better reflect the current interactions between economic activities and GDP, minimizing the influence of past economic events and policies that may no longer be relevant.

In conclusion, this methodology ensures that the model adapts to the contemporary state of the Mexican economy, thereby enhancing the relevance and precision of its predictions.

Multicollinearity

In the development process of our multiple regression model, it is crucial to assess multicollinearity between the independent variables, as its presence can affect the stability of the coefficients and the interpretation of the results. Multicollinearity occurs when two or more independent variables are highly correlated with each other, which can hinder the accurate estimation of each variable's individual effects on the dependent variable.

To detect and address multicollinearity, we use the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF), a statistical tool that measures how much the variances of the coefficients are inflated due to the correlation between the independent variables. A VIF value greater than or equal to 10 is generally considered indicative of significant multicollinearity.

The procedure followed in this analysis is as follows: we calculate the VIFs for all variables and, if the VIF of one or more variables is greater than or equal to 10, we remove the variable with the highest VIF from the model. We then recalculate the VIFs for the remaining variables and repeat this elimination process until all VIFs are below 10. This approach ensures that the variables in the model are sufficiently independent from each other, improving the stability and accuracy of the regression model.

Significance Test

The significance test of the regression is used to determine if there is a linear relationship between the response variable (in this case, National GDP) and the independent variables. The corresponding hypotheses for this test are:

  • H₀: β₁ = 0 (The regression is not significant, meaning at least one of the independent variables does not show a relationship).
  • H₁: β₁ ≠ 0 (The regression is significant, meaning the independent variables show a relationship).
Rejecting H₀ if:
  • EP > Fα,k,n-k-1

Where:

  • α is the significance level (in this case 0.05), which defines the threshold for rejecting the null hypothesis H0.
  • k is the number of predictors (independent variables) in your multiple regression model (k=9).
  • n-k-1 is the degrees of freedom associated with the error (residuals), based on the sample size n and the number of predictors k (n=60, n-k-1=50).

Test Result

In this case, our result was 182.282 > 2.0734, so we conclude that we have a significant regression, and we proceed with the analysis of the confidence intervals of the constant in our model.

Regression Through the Origin?
At this point, we will analyze the constant of the model to determine if it is appropriate to force the model to pass through the origin. The decision depends on whether the confidence interval of the constant includes zero; if so, it might be considered to eliminate the constant and perform a regression without an intercept.
The confidence intervals of the constant are: -9837427.2603 < b0 < 3510693.9333, so 0 is part of the interval. This suggests a possible regression through the origin. However, given the context of the model and the nature of the national GDP, we will not remove the constant. It is important to consider that although the variables in the model explain a large proportion of the GDP, they do not represent all the factors influencing the national economy. Assigning a value of zero to the GDP when all explanatory variables are zero would not be realistic, as it implies there are no other economic factors at play.
Therefore, we will keep the constant in the model to reflect a more complete and accurate relationship between the variables and the national GDP.
Assumptions of the Model

The next step is to ensure that the assumptions of the model residuals are met so that, if these assumptions hold, we can be confident in the reliability of the model.


1. Normality of Residuals with Zero Mean

It is essential to ensure that the model's residuals are normally distributed and have a mean close to zero. There are two approaches to verify this assumption: the graphical method and the analytical method. In this project, we will use both approaches to have both a visual and statistical confirmation of the normality of the residuals.


Graphical Method

For the graphical validation, we will use the QQ plot (quantile-quantile plot) and a histogram of the residuals. In the QQ plot, the "x" axis represents the theoretical quantiles of a normal distribution, while the "y" axis shows the observed quantiles of the residuals. A trend line is superimposed on the plot to help us visualize the spread of the points: the closer the points are to the line, the more normal the distribution of the residuals will be. Additionally, a histogram of the residuals allows us to assess the shape of their distribution and verify that it is centered around zero.


QQ Plot

In the obtained QQ plot, we can observe that the points are largely aligned with the trend line, indicating that the residuals follow a normal distribution.



Histogram

The histogram of the residuals also shows an approximately symmetric shape centered around zero. Both graphs support the validity of the assumption of normality with a mean of zero for the residuals.



Analytical Method

Complementarily, we applied the Chi-square normality test. This statistical test allows us to verify whether the distribution of the residuals significantly deviates from a normal distribution. The null hypothesis of this test states that the residuals follow a normal distribution with a mean of 0, while the alternative hypothesis indicates otherwise.

Our Chi-square test results were: 4.8779 < 11.0705. These results show that there is not enough evidence to reject the hypothesis of normality with a mean of 0 for the residuals. This analytically confirms that the residuals come from a normal distribution with a mean of zero, thereby validating one of the key assumptions for the model's reliability.


2. Constant Variance Assumption of Residuals

A second crucial assumption in linear regression is the constant variance of residuals, also known as homoscedasticity. This means that the variability of errors should be constant across all prediction values. If this assumption is not met (heteroscedasticity), it may indicate that the model struggles to fit certain regions of the data, which would affect the accuracy and validity of predictions.


Graphical Verification with the Versus Fit Plot

To assess this assumption, we use the Versus Fit Plot, a scatter plot where the "x" axis represents the model’s estimated responses, and the "y" axis shows the residuals. If the points on this graph are randomly scattered and show no clear pattern or trend, we can assume that the variance of residuals is constant. On the other hand, if the points exhibit a specific shape or pattern (e.g., a fan or cone shape), this would indicate heteroscedasticity.


Upon observing the Versus Fit Plot, we note that the points are uniformly distributed around the horizontal axis without a discernible pattern. This indicates that the residuals have constant variance across all estimates of our model, confirming that the homoscedasticity assumption holds.



3. Assumption of Uncorrelated Errors

For a linear regression model to be valid, the errors or residuals must be independent of each other. This means that the value of one error should not depend on the value of previous errors; in other words, the errors must be uncorrelated. If correlation is present, the model may be leaving unexplained patterns, which could affect the accuracy of its predictions.


Graphical Method: Versus Order Plot

One way to validate the uncorrelation of errors is through the Versus Order Plot. In this graph, the "x" axis represents the position of each observation in temporal order (1, 2, ..., n), and the "y" axis shows the value of the residuals. This graph helps identify whether the errors follow any pattern based on time or observation order. If no clear pattern or trend is observed in the dispersion of points, we can assume that the errors are uncorrelated.


Upon observing the Versus Order Plot, we note that no discernible pattern or obvious trend is present in the residuals over time, which suggests that the errors are uncorrelated. For this reason, we conclude that the assumption of uncorrelated errors is met based on the graphical analysis.



Analytical Method: Durbin-Watson Test

To complement the validation of this assumption, we used the Durbin-Watson test, which is a statistical test that detects the presence of first-order autocorrelation in the errors of a regression model. This test is useful in contexts where errors are recorded at regular time intervals and can confirm whether there is correlation between consecutive errors.


In this case, the results of the Durbin-Watson test were inconclusive, so we relied on the visual analysis obtained from the Versus Order Plot. Since the graph does not show any pattern of correlation, we conclude that the assumption of uncorrelated errors holds.

Outlier Detection

Identifying outliers is crucial in regression analysis, as they can negatively influence the fit and accuracy of the model. To address this issue, we applied a standardized residual method, which allows us to assess if any observation deviates significantly from the general pattern of the data.


Method Description

The standardized residual method involves calculating an adjusted residual for each observation, taking into account its influence on the model. In this way, we can detect unusual observations. In this analysis, an observation is considered an outlier if its absolute standardized residual exceeds a value of 3.


Detection Process
  • Calculation of Standardized Residuals: The standardized residual for each observation is calculated.
  • Outlier Detection Criterion: Observations with an absolute standardized residual greater than 3 are considered outliers.


In this case, the analysis did not detect any outliers, indicating that all observations are within an acceptable range of variability. This means there are no extreme values that could distort the interpretation of the model.

The absence of outliers allows us to state that the model is stable and reliable, adequately representing the relationship between the variables without being affected by unusual data points.

Model Conclusion

After conducting a thorough analysis of our regression model, we evaluated and verified each of the fundamental assumptions: normality of residuals, constant variance, independence of errors, and absence of outliers. Each of these steps contributed to confirming that our model meets the necessary statistical requirements to be considered robust and reliable.

The absence of patterns or trends in the residuals and the confirmation of constant variance indicate that the model consistently captures the relationship between the variables. Additionally, the verification of uncorrelated errors and the lack of outlier observations reinforce that the model is not distorted by external or unusual influences in the data.

Taken together, these results validate that our model is statistically solid and suitable for making reliable predictions, offering a high degree of accuracy and performance in its practical application.

Conclusion
In this analysis, the selected 9-variable regression model provides an optimal solution for estimating the National GDP (Ŷ) based on key economic sectors. This model outperforms others tested, demonstrating a superior performance with an Adjusted R² of 96.51% and an R² of 97.04%, indicating that it explains nearly all of the variability in GDP growth. The model includes the following economic sectors: Agriculture, Generation, Transmission and Distribution of Electrical Energy, Construction, Beverage and Tobacco Industry, Machinery and Equipment Manufacturing, Chemical Product Manufacturing, Health and Social Assistance Services, Educational Services, and Temporary Accommodation Services. These sectors were identified as the most significant contributors to national GDP growth.

Each variable's positive or negative impact on GDP is represented in the model's equation, with specific coefficients showing the degree to which each sector influences GDP growth. For example, the positive coefficients for sectors such as Construction (X₃) and Health and Social Assistance Services (X₇) reflect their significant contributions to national economic expansion, while the negative coefficient for Chemical Product Manufacturing (X₆) suggests a less pronounced impact.

Through careful validation, the model satisfies all statistical assumptions, including normality of residuals, constant variance, independence of errors, and absence of outliers. This ensures the robustness and reliability of the model for making informed predictions about Mexico's economic performance.

Furthermore, the model's accuracy was confirmed through a comparison of predicted values with actual GDP data, showing a strong alignment with real-world trends. The lack of significant patterns or trends in residuals further supports the model's credibility.

In conclusion, the selected model offers a highly reliable and precise tool for estimating GDP growth and understanding the dynamics of key economic sectors. It provides valuable insights for policymakers and economists seeking to predict and manage national economic performance. By leveraging this model, stakeholders can make data-driven decisions that foster sustainable economic development and enhance strategic planning.
